The mark bands — where does your essay sit?

Paper 2 is the same paper for SL and HL. It has two sections. Section A asks you to respond to one thematic study (Development & Sustainability in this case). Section B asks an integrating question that requires you to link across all three thematic studies and the core topics. Each question is marked out of 15.

1–4 marks
Limited
  • Descriptive rather than analytical
  • Arguments poorly structured or unclear
  • Little relevant knowledge present
  • Claims unsupported by evidence or examples
  • No engagement with counterclaims
Avoid: listing facts about the IMF without explaining what they reveal about power or legitimacy.
5–9 marks
Adequate
  • Some understanding of the question's demands
  • Structured but lacks coherence or depth
  • Examples mentioned but underdeveloped
  • Limited justification of claims
  • Diverse perspectives not fully explored
Common issue: describing what happened in Sri Lanka without explaining what it reveals about sovereignty or structural power.
10–12 marks
Good
  • Demands of question understood and addressed
  • Well-structured with coherent arguments
  • Relevant examples developed and linked to claims
  • Conceptual understanding demonstrated throughout
  • Counterclaims explored and evaluated
To push higher: explicitly name concepts and explain why your example matters, not just what happened.
13–15 marks
Excellent
  • All demands of the question addressed, including implications
  • Accurate knowledge used to strengthen arguments, not just illustrate them
  • Strong conceptual grasp — concepts integrated, not bolted on
  • All main points justified; arguments fully coherent
  • Counterclaims genuinely engaged with, not just acknowledged
Top marks require synthesis: show how your Sri Lanka / Kenya / climate justice cases work together to build a nuanced argument.
Key terms and concepts to deploy

The examiner expects conceptual language woven into your argument — not listed at the start. Use these terms where they genuinely sharpen your point.

Power
Structural powerSusan Strange — the ability to shape the rules others must follow (e.g. the IMF setting conditionality terms)
Hard powerCoercive economic leverage — withholding loans, imposing austerity conditions
Soft powerUsing development finance to build relationships and influence — arguably China's BRI strategy
HegemonyDominance sustained through institutions and norms, not just force — the Washington Consensus as hegemonic ideology
Sovereignty
Conditional sovereigntySovereignty constrained by loan conditions — states remain formally sovereign but policy choices are externally determined
Economic sovereigntyA state's ability to control its own fiscal and monetary policy — eroded by IMF conditionality
Debt-trap diplomacyContested term — the claim that lenders deliberately create debt dependency to extract strategic concessions (e.g. Hambantota Port)
Legitimacy
Democratic legitimacyDo IMF/World Bank decisions reflect the interests of those they affect? Weighted voting gives richer states disproportionate influence
Output legitimacyLegitimacy derived from effective outcomes — if structural adjustment produces growth, is that sufficient justification?
Contested legitimacyKenya's parliament rejection of IMF conditions as an act of asserting popular legitimacy over international institutional authority
Interdependence
Asymmetric interdependenceInterdependence that benefits one party more — Global South states need capital; lenders hold leverage
Complex interdependenceKeohane & Nye — multiple channels of connection reduce the usefulness of force, but economic power still dominates
DependencyA structural condition, not just a policy choice — some states are locked into borrowing regardless of government preferences
Theoretical frameworks — how to use them in your essay

Theory in Paper 2 is a tool, not a label. Don't just name a theory — use it to explain why something happened and what it reveals. SL students are expected to demonstrate conceptual understanding; HL students must apply frameworks analytically in Paper 2 and use them to structure independently researched case studies in Paper 3.

SL + HL
Dependency Theory
Frank, Prebisch, Cardoso
The global economic system is structured to keep peripheral (developing) states dependent on core (wealthy) states. Trade and finance reinforce this — they don't resolve it.
Apply to: IMF conditionality that opens developing markets to foreign goods, undermining local industry. The "revolving door" of repeated IMF loans. Zambia and Sri Lanka as peripheral states.
Challenge it with: China's BRI shows dependency isn't exclusively Western — or that South–South finance replicates the same dynamics.
HL focus
World-Systems Theory
Wallerstein
The world is divided into a core, semi-periphery and periphery. Finance flows from core to periphery with interest, locking in inequality. Development within the system is constrained by design.
Apply to: Structural adjustment as a mechanism for maintaining core–periphery relations. The $2.5 trillion SDG financing gap as evidence of systemic under-investment.
Challenge it with: South Korea, Singapore and China as counter-examples of periphery states that escaped the system — though some argue this was exceptional.
SL + HL
Liberal Institutionalism
Keohane, Nye, IMF institutional view
International institutions like the IMF and World Bank enable cooperation, reduce risk, and can promote development — if properly governed. Interdependence creates mutual incentives for responsible behaviour.
Apply to: The SDG 17 framework as an example of institutionalised cooperation. Min Zhu's TED argument for managed interdependence.
Challenge it with: Who controls the institutions? Weighted voting means wealthy states shape the rules. Institutions can embed power asymmetries, not neutralise them.
HL focus
Structural Power
Susan Strange
True power in global politics is the ability to shape the structures within which others make decisions — control of finance, production, security, and knowledge. The US and IMF exercise structural power over borrowing states.
Apply to: IMF conditionality as structural power — states are not forced to borrow, but the conditions they accept shape all subsequent policy choices. Washington Consensus as a knowledge structure.
Challenge it with: Kenya's rejection of IMF terms suggests structural power has limits — states can refuse, at a cost.
Connecting your own case studies — climate justice & global governance

Paper 2 Section B asks integrating questions that require you to connect across thematic studies. Your own researched case studies on climate justice and global governance can strengthen both Section A and Section B responses — here's how.

🌍
Climate Justice
The debt cycle intersects directly with climate vulnerability. Countries with the highest debt burdens are often those most exposed to climate impacts (flooding, drought, crop failure) — yet they have the least fiscal space to invest in adaptation or green transition. This is a justice argument about who pays for a crisis they did not cause.
Link to these videos:
SDGs explainer (Section 5) → the $2.5tn financing gap includes climate adaptation finance
IMF debt trap (Section 2) → austerity cuts environmental budgets first
Sri Lanka case (Section 3) → economic collapse accelerates environmental degradation
Essay angle: "To what extent does the global finance system inhibit climate justice for developing states?" Use your own case study + Sri Lanka + SDG gap as a three-point argument.
🏛️
Global Governance
Who governs global finance — and in whose interests? The IMF, World Bank, G20, and WTO all play roles, but their decision-making structures are weighted toward wealthy states. Your global governance case study can interrogate whether these institutions have the legitimacy to impose conditions on sovereign states.
Link to these videos:
The IMF explainer (Section 1) → who controls voting shares?
Grieve Chelwa (Section 2) → governance as a mechanism of structural power
Kenya rejection (Section 4) → state resistance as a challenge to governance legitimacy
Essay angle: "Evaluate the claim that global financial governance lacks legitimacy." Use your case study + Kenya + Min Zhu's counter-argument to build a balanced evaluation.
✏️
Section B integrating tip
Section B questions require you to link across Development & Sustainability, Rights & Justice, and Peace & Conflict. The debt cycle is a strong thread across all three: it shapes what rights states can afford to protect (health, education), creates conditions for political instability, and raises fundamental questions of global justice. When you see a Section B question, ask: how does the debt/finance angle connect to the other thematic studies?
Possible cross-thematic links:
D&S → Rights & Justice: Austerity cuts health and education spending — what does this mean for economic and social rights?
D&S → Peace & Conflict: Economic collapse driven by debt can fuel political instability and conflict (Sri Lanka 2022 protests; Zambia unrest)
All three: Climate finance debt locks vulnerable states into a cycle where they cannot invest in prevention, adaptation, or post-conflict recovery
HL only · Paper 3
Preparing your independently researched case studies
Paper 3 is a stimulus-based paper where you respond to unseen material using your own researched case studies on Global Political Challenges. For the debt/finance topic, the most directly relevant GPCs are Poverty, Environment, and Health. Your case studies should go significantly deeper than the videos — use these clips as orientation, then build your own evidence base. Here's what Paper 3 examiners expect your case studies to include:
  • A specific, named example with precise factual detail — not "a developing country" but "Sri Lanka, 2022" or "Zambia's sovereign default, 2020"
  • A GPC lens applied — how does your case illustrate the GPC you've researched? What does it reveal about the political challenge?
  • At least one theoretical framework applied analytically — Wallerstein, Strange, or dependency theory for this topic area
  • Multiple actor perspectives — state, IGO, civil society, and ideally a Global South voice (e.g. Chelwa's argument, Kenya's parliament)
  • A contested conclusion — what does the evidence suggest, and what remains genuinely debated? Paper 3 rewards intellectual honesty about complexity
  • A connection to core concepts — Power, Sovereignty, Legitimacy, or Interdependence should be visible in your analysis, not just named
Before you write — examiner checklist
✓ Do this (SL + HL)
  • Directly answer the question in your opening — state your argument clearly
  • Use concepts (power, sovereignty, legitimacy, interdependence) as analytical tools, not decorations
  • Develop each case study: what happened, why it matters, what concept it illustrates
  • Include a genuine counterclaim — engage with it, don't just list it
  • Synthesise at the end: what does the weight of evidence suggest?
  • Reference diverse perspectives — Global South voices alongside institutional ones
  • HL additionally: name and apply a theoretical framework explicitly; link to your Paper 3 GPC case studies in Section B responses
✗ Avoid this (SL + HL)
  • Describing what happened without explaining what it reveals about global politics
  • Naming a theory without applying it to your evidence
  • Using the same case study for every point — examiners want range
  • Treating the question as settled — the best essays acknowledge genuine complexity
  • Ignoring the sustainability dimension — this is a D&S question, not just development
  • Quoting a video directly — paraphrase and attribute the argument
  • HL: don't use your Paper 3 case studies as decoration — they should anchor your argument, not illustrate it
